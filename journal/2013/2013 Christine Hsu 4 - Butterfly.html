Nov. 15, 2013

Butterfly

I never thought to be able to do butterfly since it is considered the toughest among four swimming styles. After having practiced at full throttle over six months, I finally could do it as easily as I do the other three styles—backstroke, freestyle and breaststroke.

I used to feel envious badly every time I saw swimmers doing butterfly. Six months ago, I finally decided to try it myself. Without a coach, I could only watch films posted by excellent swimmers on the Internet. When I got some hints, I could not wait to try them.

Every morning, I quickly finished the 2000-meter swim and eagerly tried those tips to see if they worked. I carefully observed my arms and legs to see if I did exactly the same as shown in the film. From dolphin kick, pulling water, pushing head, to swinging arms, I got improved little by little. After one or two months, I finally could swim for 25 meters without stopping despite a minor problem of breathing.

It was hard to learn from films on the internet. I thought to myself, “How do I breathe when I do other styles?” I began to observe how I breathed and imitated it when I did butterfly. “Practice makes perfect,” I got the hint! After I could breathe smoothly, I found the rhythm and eventually, swimming over 1000 meters was no longer a problem.

Butterfly is a unique style combining breaststroke and freestyle. I especially enjoy gliding while I pull and swing my arms to the front, like a bird opening wide its wings in the sky—graceful and elegant. One of my friends told me that the way I swam was called fish-swim. Despite my over 15 years of swimming, little did I know that people could swim like a fish. I surfed the Internet for the term and found out the related idea that the more relaxed you feel when you swim, the easier you’ll be able to do it. Swimming like a fish softens my body and saves my energy, which enables me to swim for as long a time as I want to.

“Why do I like to swim so much?” My friends feel it incredible that I go swimming nearly every day for over one decade. Swimming energizes me and nurtures in me the perseverance to overcome difficulties at work or in life. I believe that any sport could do the same, but swimming is the only kind that one could do with “no sweat.”
