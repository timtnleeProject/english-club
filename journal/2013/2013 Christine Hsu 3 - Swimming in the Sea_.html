Oct. 4, 2013

Swimming in the Sea

“Ten, nine, eight … three, two, one!” Three thousand people in swimming suits or wetsuits counted together at the parking lot of Wai-mu Shan beach in the morning of June 30. Right after the starting gun went off, all of us walked in groups into the sea to challenge the 4000-meter open water swimming. With a rescue tube on my back, I followed my group members slipping into the cold water, swimming to the sea.

In the first 10 minutes, I could still see my group members when I turned back, but soon, they all disappeared. I was totally alone! To conquer my fear, I tried hard to closely follow someone in front of me. Having other swimmers’ rescue tubes around made me feel safe and relaxed. I began to observe the other swimmers.

We all put on the same swimming caps, but some participants particularly wore a zebra hat or tied balloons on their heads. Some banks and famous companies made a big balloon with a logo on it as a floating advertisement. With the balloons, rescue tubes, and special hats, the sea looked so colorful.

It seemed dangerous to swim in the sea, but with lifesavers on our

right hand side to guide the way, I could stop any time to enjoy cucumbers and view the splendid shorelines on my left hand side, on which there were some exotic coffee shops and white castles. Swimming in the sea was all very interesting to me until I ran into a reef rock.

After a short break, I felt so relaxed that I almost ran into a reef rock. As it was too big for me to escape, I immediately stretched my arms to keep a certain distance from it and touched the stone lightly to push my body up. When I tried to jump over the rock, I saw a deep ditch next to the rock. Without thinking, I jumped into that ditch and swiftly raised my head above the sea surface to inhale fresh air. What a thrilling experience I’ve ever had.

Right after I jumped over that rock, I saw various fishes swimming in groups. Those finger-long blue or golden fish under my feet looked like small lights shining everywhere. I could not stop admiring such wonderful scenery until I saw garbage, bottles and plastic bags floating everywhere. When swinging my arms harder to clear the way, I felt like electricity was piercing through my right palm but saw nothing on it. I was not aware that Jellyfish had just kissed my hand until I got a swollen palm the next day.

I kept swimming for several hundred meters before some boats appeared on my right hand side, on which lifesavers were waving hands to point the way. We swam to the left side orderly. Soon, I saw a welcome balloon-door on the beach about 500 meters away. “Bravo!” I cried out excitedly. “There’s the destination!”

The last 500 meters was quite difficult for me as I swam almost all alone. “After I finish the last cucumber, I’ll swim to the end without resting.” I told myself while chewing the icy cucumber. I continuously peddled to the beach just like I did in the pool every morning. When I felt too tired to keep going, I found sand, instead of fish, under my feet and the water was getting shallow. I bent my knees, stood up and walked to the welcome balloon-door. The host looked at me and yelled, “Congratulations! You’ve made it!”

From this experience of swimming in the sea, I discovered something about myself: my attitudes to swimming and work are the same—I like to work alone. Even when I work with a group of people, I am eager to take the lead. This activity, however, makes me realize that no matter how strong I am, I still need partners to help achieve this goal.
