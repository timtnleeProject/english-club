Apr. 4, 2009

My Father

My father was born in a humble family in 1912 in the suburb of Shao-xing, Zhe-jiang Province. Although he had studied just one and a half years, he could write well. At the age of 14, he worked as a vender to sell snacks in front of theaters to support his family. One of my grandfather’s friends took pity on my dad, who he thought should not have worked in such a complicated place at so early an age, and arranged my dad to be an apprentice at an electrical mechanical store.

My dad was handsome, smart, humorous, honest, brave, cautious but a little hot-tempered. Within less than three years, my dad was able to manage and solve any problems related to electric mechanical things. At the turn of the 20th century, nearly everyone was poor in China, so while my father rode a bicycle and rang the bell all the way home, many neighbors went to the street and yelled, “Light Wen-biaw (my father’s name) is coming home.”

It was after my dad passed the ROC armed forces at Jian Bridge in Hang-chou City that he finally left the store where he had worked for a long period of time. In the military, my father was responsible for building and mending electrical equipment. In the 1930s, a commanding officer assigned my dad another mission: to drive a special car to light the track for airplanes to land at night.

Not long after, the 8-year war against Japan broke out at Lugou Bridge. On Aug. 14, 1937, the Japanese bombed the Jian Bridge air force military base. Our air fighters met them in the air and struck down six enemy battle planes. Because my father participated in this famous and daring battle for ground service, he got a medal.

At the risk of his life, my father transported the light tracks and some other strategic substances safely to the rear area of Chong-qing successfully, after passing through the narrow mountain trails under enemy’s canon fire and gun shooting.

All his life, my father was loyal to his family, country and friends and cared little about fame and wealth. After retiring from the military service, his old friends often came to our house for dinner or just for chatting. Hearing them laughing, I knew they must have shared a good memory of their youth.

My father had a neat, well-equipped little box with a variety of tools. If our friends or neighbors had any problems with electrical devices or wanted to set them up, my father would go to their houses with his tool box to work. Though sometimes returning home exhausted, my father took delight in his work and never got tired of it until he got serious asthma.

A young man named Steve living next door was impressed by my father’s excellent ability and asked my father to take him as an apprentice. My father was very happy to teach all he knew to this young guy. Up to now, Steve is deeply grateful for my father’s teaching.

My father had strived to the fullest in his life. I cannot say enough in praise of him.
