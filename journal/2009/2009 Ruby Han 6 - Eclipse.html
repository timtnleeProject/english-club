Oct. 8, 2009

Eclipse

Are you able to identify Pictures 1 and 2 below? I took them to Mucha's Wenshan Park on the morning of July 22nd. And what’s special about that day? It was the day when the longest solar eclipse of the 21st century was to be witnessed in India, Mainland China, and even Taiwan. With the special glasses, we were able to see only one sun eclipsed in the sky, but why did we see a lot of new moons reflected on the ground as shown in Picture 2?

Accidentally, I saw a woman on TV saying, “If you couldn’t see the eclipse in the sky, you can look at the ground under trees.” I ran to the park in no time and was so excited to see a lot of new-moon shades on the ground. My husband told me those shapes were made by the sun shining through the chinks among leaves. Physicists named them “Pinhole Imagining” (針孔成像).

The other day, I went to the park again. At the same place and the same time, 10:04 a.m., I saw different things on the ground. All shades under the trees became circles of different sizes (as shown in Picture 3). Certainly, you cannot see the new-moon images beneath the trees again until the next total solar eclipse to happen in 2070, that is, 60 years later.
