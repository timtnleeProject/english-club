Oct. 2, 2015

Joining an International Conference

My experience of joining the Reach to Recovery International Breast Cancer Support Conference four years ago in Taipei was wonderful, but the one hosted by the Chinese Cancer Rehabilitation Society (CCRS) during Sep. 6-9, 2015, at the Beijing International Conference Center seemed to me quite a disaster.

If I had not been obliged to attend this conference, I would never have thought of visiting China since rudeness and impoliteness is my general impression of the people there. Many of my friends had the experience of working with mainland Chinese whose mindlessness caused all sorts of troubles. After joining the four-day meeting with about 600 mainland Chinese, I felt much worse about their behavior. Regardless of the warning sign, they kept talking on the cell phone instead of putting it on vibration. I was furiously annoyed by the noise caused by the Chinese participants. What’s even worse, they left the conference before it was finished and snatched all the desserts during the break time. To make sure the 60 foreign delegates have a fair chance to enjoy the refreshments, the organizer was forced to set up for them a particular area for the tea and coffee breaks. How ridiculous was that!

Despite the limited number of good papers, I was greatly inspired by one presentation made by Yuhong from China—“Research on the Application of ‘Pink Lady’ WeChat Group to the Management of Patients with Breast Cancer after Discharge.” The patients discharged from the hospital Yuhong worked for were asked to join a WeChat group through which they could get the information as well as advice related to breast cancer. Yuhong and other volunteers took turns to answer the patients’ questions or to track their health. This kind of instant service was very helpful to patients. In fact, I had a similar peer group networking service, too.

Around five months ago, one of my boss’s friends came to share with us an app, Line@. Unlike the Line group, all members of the Line@ group are not aware of each other’s identity. Even I, the founder of the group, hardly know my members except when I receive a message or sticker from an individual. The most interesting part was when I sent out batch messages or pictures, all other members would think I sent them individually. Another function that greatly attracted me was that all members could use one-on-one chat to communicate with each other. I created this group about three months ago and have ever since invited new patients and survivors to attend classes or activities held by our alliance. So far I have got 110 members. Whenever I read something which might be useful to them, I would take pictures and send them along with my brief observations and it was really wonderful to get their affirmation or even just a sticker. Besides, the survivors always feel more comfortable to ask questions on the Internet. After creating this group, I got more chances to solve the patients’ problems, which made me really happy since I did not need to go to the hospital to visit the patients but still got the good work done.

Yuhong’s presentation encourages me a great deal not only because we actually provided the similar program for survivors, but also because it boosts my self-confidence, making me believe that someday I will be able to also make a presentation to share with survivors around the world about how I have used networking to communicate with and help patients in Taiwan.

The trip to Beijing in September was in general a disappointment to me, but I was extremely pleased to meet those delegates from Australia, Malaysia, Africa and America who had previously joined the conference in Taipei. And I finally got to visit the world-famous Forbidden City, which I had seen so many times only on TV. It was like a huge maze. Without the tour guide, I might have gotten lost. I must say this is probably one of the best cultural tours in China I have ever enjoyed.
