Nov. 28, 2015

One, Two, Three

I counted “One,” my blind partner, Sam, counted “Two,” and then we counted “Three” and pedaled our tandem at the same time. Besides pedaling and controlling direction, I also had to report to Sam what I saw all the way from Bei-Tou to the Taipei Grand Mosque.

“Are you interested in accompanying the blind on bicycling?” One of my good friends asked me last month. I raised my doubt curiously: “How do they ride a bike if they can’t see?”

“That’s exactly why they need our company.”

“But I don’t have confidence in riding with the blind sitting behind me.”

“Your partner will pedal with you. Don’t worry,” My friend comforted me.

On the 22nd of November, a beautiful sunny day, I took part in the special riding. It was the first time for me to ride a bike for 50 kilometers as well as to ride the same bike with a blind. As a green hand, I arrived 30 minutes earlier to practice riding the tandem first by myself and then with Sam before departure. When I first knew that my partner would be a male, I did wonder if I could carry a man taller and heavier than me. (When we registered, we were asked to provide personal data about height and weight.) Fortunately, Sam was not born blind. Before he lost his sight, he had been an amateur biker. He taught me when and how to change the speed. Riding a 20-speed derailleur bike, 50 kilometers seemed not a big deal to me but my bottom got extremely sore afterwards.

On the way to the Taipei Grand Mosque, we rested twice. It would be good to get off the bike, drink some water and chat with other volunteers, but it was hard for me to relax with a blind standing beside me. I could not leave Sam even for only one minute. For the sake of safety, all blind people were taught to stay close to the volunteers, who rode the same bike with them, during the whole journey. And when I sat, he also had to sit so he could easily sense when I was to move.

There were lots of surprises during the whole ride. The one that surprised me most was Jim, the other blind. He was a professor of Tamkang University, and he liked swimming. How could he swim without bumping into others? He enlightened me by saying that sometimes the coach would clear the way for him. If not, he could share the same lane with others as the water flow would help him to know how close he was to the swimmer in front of him. He told me that a well-trained blind could do many things beyond my imagination. For example, he could walk alone at Tamkang University where there were lots of hills and stairs. If I did not know Jim, I would not believe a blind could do so independently.

From this fresh and unforgettable ride, I’ve learned a lot. For example, we must call out the name of the blind we wish to talk to, or else all blinds present may respond together. Instead of pulling a blind’s hand to signal him to move with us, we should lightly touch their hands so as not to shock them. Most special and interesting of all, when we introduce the scenery around us or the dishes on the table, we have to say, “Around the three o’clock position is a church!” Or “Beef noodle in the nine o’clock position!”

How terrible it must be to live in a dark world! I’m glad to be able to accompany them to enjoy life. Whenever I confront difficulties in my life, I would think of them: If they can live bravely and optimistically regardless of their disability. Why can’t I?
