Nov. 21, 2015

Together We Can Make a Difference

“Before I tell you how to do the fund raising, I want everyone to paint a flying horse.” Usha, a consultant from a Singaporean management company, said to us in the opening of her speech.

The speech was made in a 2-day advocacy forum discussing advanced breast cancer held by Novartis last week. We had 27 delegates from eight countries--America, Hong Kong, Indonesia, Malaysia, Singapore, South Africa, Thailand and Taiwan. I was assigned by my boss to participate in it with eight of my colleagues. During the two-day gathering, these specialists shared with us how they had helped breast cancer patients, which certainly has benefits both individuals and organizations involved in Taiwan.

Fundraising was the most difficult task for every non-profit organization, so Novartis particularly invited Usha to share her successful experience. “What does a flying horse look like?” We each began to think after we grabbed a piece of paper to paint. All flying horses we drew looked the same, with their wings, legs and tails. Usha walked around to check our paintings and then she showed us a slide which was astonishing! On the slide, all horses were flying with no wings. One horse stood on a blanket, one was tied with balloons, and another sat in a hot air balloon. Then Usha asked us, “What do you think of when you do the fund raising?” “Money,” we all have the same answer. “Fundraising is not about money.” Usha surprised me again by saying so. She continuously told us some experiences about how she raised funds without asking for money at the beginning but got lots of it at the end. She gave us a brand new idea, an idea completely unheard of.

Another speech made in the forum about consultant skill also impressed me. The speaker advised us that when we talked to the patients, it was all right to cry with them. The view sounded unorthodox to me: I always held back my tears until I left the patients as I had been taught to always appear optimistic and cheerful when with patients. The speaker, however, explained, “It’s a good way to show your compassion.”

With only 27 participants in this conference, we got lots of time to share our opinions with each other and the speakers. It was great to learn about different experiences from other leaders of survivors groups or foundations, but I also regretted how insufficient my English ability was. Most of the time, I could not respond to their sharing as I did not understand them, especially when the talk was long. It frustrated me badly.

Despite a lack of confidence in my English, I still accompanied nine delegates during their visit to a night market right after the conference. Four of them were super shoppers who persisted in walking through every alley of Wu-Fen-Pu (五分埔) while other delegates retreated back to their hotel after having dinner. When the night ended, I got extremely exhausted but as I thought about just how comfortable and safe they must have felt to be accompanied by a local, then it all seemed worthwhile.
