Aug. 11, 2023

Guide Runner

July 25, 2023, was a memorable day for me. On that very day, I rushed home after getting off work, prepared dinner quickly, and then headed for Dr. Sun Yat-Sen Memorial Hall to serve as a guide runner volunteer — a volunteer work I had never tried before.

As I arrived there, other volunteers and the visually impaired runners were all amazed that I actually knew about them, and they were curious why I would want to join them. I told them it was all God’s arrangement. Over the past six years of early morning runs, I had never seen two runners run together with a rope in their hands until last June. Eagerly, I began to search on the Internet and discovered that one of them was a sighted runner, while the other was blind. An idea popped out of my mind: I could help the blind run, too. Therefore, I searched deeply and found 14 groups formed by visually impaired people who loved to run. Among these groups, in different cities around Taiwan, the one near the Dr. Sun Yat-Sen Memorial Hall was most suitable for me. After a happy chat with the group leader, I decided to join them once a week.

On my first day, I was supposed to be the leader’s guide runner so that he could teach me how to guide and support blind runners, but due to the shortage of volunteers, I had to also guide another blind runner named Yuki while the leader was teaching me how to provide verbal cues and navigation support. Both the leader and Yuki had amblyopia, so they could see people right in front of them and would hardly run into pedestrians. I only needed to caution them when the road ahead was not flat. With a rope in our hands, I could instruct Yuki when to turn left or right or when to run behind me to avoid a crowd. Yuki had joined this group for over three years, so she was able to share some tips with me to help her run safely. That night, I felt that they were more like my guide runners than I was theirs.

The second time, I guided a lady who was born blind. She told me how badly she had been treated by her grandmother until she began to earn money by working as a massage therapist. I also shared with her the miserable childhood experience of my daughter as a kid with a cleft lip. The lady appeared surprised but was empathetic to such children’s grievances.

The third runner I guided was Chi-Zon. She lost her sight in an accident, and her boyfriend also suffered from amblyopia. We talked a lot during the five-kilometer

run. She told me that a guide runner was allowed to attend marathons free. That was great news for me, for I really hated spending money on running. After serving as a guide runner three times, I finally got to join their Line group. I received numerous warm greetings and welcome messages from group members that very night.

Up till today, I am still amazed by the fact that they always chat with me with a smile, and that all volunteers are friendly and enthusiastic. Running with them is so enjoyable. When running or doing volunteer work, I am alone most of the time and inevitably feel lonely. Therefore, I will seize this great opportunity and have their company as much as I can. I think I will join them, not just once, but twice, a week.
