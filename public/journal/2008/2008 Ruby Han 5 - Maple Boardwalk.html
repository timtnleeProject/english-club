Oct. 21, 2008

Maple Boardwalk

The beautiful Maple Boardwalk about one kilometer long is one of ChengChi University’s so-called eight great sceneries. Due to the clean quaint boardwalk and the tall maples on its both sides, many people enjoy walking there, including me.

At the end of June, I found a lot of falling maple leaves on the road. And in July, all the trees stood there without a single leaf on them. The maples were over fifteen years old, whereas the boardwalk was finished just a few years ago, so a number of people suspected that the boardwalk must be the chief culprit. The trees became dried up because those boardwalks prevented the roots of maples from getting fresh air and water. I tried to find some administrative officials at school

to tell them this problem but in vain. It was summer vacation and on one was on duty.

With a lot of worries in mind, I went there again. It was a stunning sight that tugged on every tree as a warning sign which said, “Under prevention of insect pests—DO NOT TOUCH IT.” Obviously, the authorities had noticed and started to deal with the trees’ health problem. Unbelievably, just one month later, all of the withering maple trees turned green again with new leaves growing exuberantly. It is such a relief to see all the maple trees recovering their fine living and graceful appearances.

With great admiration, I wondered who had so mighty an ability to save all of the dying trees. The botanical expert who had saved the maple tree must also be able to save the banyans wound about by the Japanese dodder (菟絲子). From the students of CCU I found a Mr. Lian at the CCU office.

The expert Mr. Lien told me that the maples suffered from worm eating while the banyans were sojourned by Japanese dodders. They sprayed at midnight all of the maples with insecticide to kill the worms, but it would not work on the parasitic plant like Japanese dodder. There is no choice but to cut down all of the branches being twined around and burn all of them into ashes. In fact, it is too difficult and immense a task to perform because lots of polluted banyans are very tall and they grow either in a vast area or in a private garden. Fortunately, the Japanese dodders as parasites won’t kill banyans on account of themselves. They are wise enough to know if their host is dead they are going to die too. Now, all banyan trees twined around by dodders are still well alive everywhere in Taiwan.
