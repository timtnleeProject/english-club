Apr. 29, 2011

A Feast of Art

One hour before I left for home, a young lady stepped into my office. “I am coming for an interview,” she said to me smilingly. Leading her to the meeting room and delivering her a glass of water, I asked her, “Do you apply for the position of a designer or copywriter?”

“Neither,” she said. “I’ve come for the secretary job.” I got confused as my boss had not informed me of the vacancy, and I had never heard that we needed another secretary besides me.

The young lady, Hana, was a Japanese major who graduated from some university eight months ago. Without any working experience, she got the job here because her cousin was one of my boss’s good friends. Thanks to her, I no longer needed to serve tea for our visiting clients. And she also helped clean the meeting room every morning. The greatest gain I had from her employment was that my boss would ask Hana instead of me to buy her lunch so that now I could really enjoy my lunch without being interrupted by my boss.

Last Friday, we had an outing to the National Palace Museum and Taipei Art Museum to view Chagall’s and Monet’s paintings. Usually, I had to make the schedule, buy tickets, and do all the odd jobs. Now Hana took it over. I just needed to remind her not to forget about it. Because of her, I could admire the two great painters’ work wholeheartedly.

Among the two artists, I like Monet more. The main reason was that his paintings were more interesting and full of surprises. I liked to view his paintings from different angles to get different images. The other reason I prefer Money to Chagall was that Chagall mostly drew about his wives, love stories, and the people of his country, but I liked better scenes of nature.

In his later life, Monet devoted himself to painting water lilies. Besides water lilies, I liked the water plants, too, especially those in the painting “The Boat.” Among his works, I loved “Poplars on the Epte” (艾普特河岸的白楊樹) most. From it, I saw an unlimited road with white poplars and a river as blue as the sky. The picturesque shadow of poplars, river and blue sky fully caught my eyes. It was so real that I felt I had been in Monet’s garden.

We had got two volunteer guides to introduce these two artists’ works for us. Without their informative instruction, it would have been difficult for me to appreciate Monet and Chagall. A friend of mine who works at Taipei Art Museum as a volunteer told me that before the art exhibition, those guides needed to attend training classes, read piles of papers, and memorize abundant information by heart. I did appreciate their selfless dedication.
