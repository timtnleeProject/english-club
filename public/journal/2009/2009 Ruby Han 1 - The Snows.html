The Snows

I really don’t know why I was so obsessed with snow. For the sake of seeing snow, I would make all possible efforts.

About 25 years ago, on a winter vacation, I first got up to Hehuan Mountain with my husband and some of our friends. We met hardship and unpleasantness such as long distance mountain climbing, coarse inns and a large crowd. Worst of all, there was no snow at all but just some ice on the side of the road and ice bars hung down the eaves.

Almost ten years later, with strong will, we brought our children to visit it again. Trapped in the heavy traffic jam, we drove our car equipped with iron chins to keep it from sliding down from the upward slope of the muddy road. Finally, we got to the top of a mountain covered with snow. A huge number of people were already there. My children were very excited; they sat on the big plastic bag used as the snow sled to slide down from the higher spot to the lower place. To the people who live in the subtropical area, the snow was very attractive to them. Despite the difficult travel in snow, we still felt satisfied. (Picture 1)

Accidentally, I got the news of snow on the radio. The weatherman reported that it would snow if the weather got cold enough at Bamboo Lake in the Green Mountain, or Youngmingshan. I was so excited and called the Bamboo Lake police headquarters to check the weather since a cold current was passing through the northern island from the mainland. Once I got the good news I immediately begged my husband to drive me there to see my dreamy snow. Although he was reluctant, we finally drove up to Green Mountain very early in the morning. (Picture 2)

Unexpectedly, a lot of cars were creeping along the steep narrow road covered with morning mist. The higher we got, the brighter the day was. It was a surprise that some cars heading downhill were decorated with a snowman or a pile of snow on their top. With great excitement, we arrived at the Bamboo Lake and saw the thin white snow sparkling over the grass in the morning daylight. Surrounded by the light of snow, we felt happy and satisfied.

Almost eight years ago, we took a trip to Japan during the lunar New Year holiday. Once we arrived in Tokyo, we saw some snowflakes flying in the sky. The next morning, not long after we arrived at the foot of Mountain Ho Cau Lake, the snow was getting heavier and heavier. About one hour later, the snow accumulated nearly half a meter tall. You really couldn’t imagine how thick, how soft, how tiny, how beautiful the snow was. We played the snowball fighting, burying our feet and body in the snow or sitting in the plastic tub to slide down from the slope (Picture 3). Three days later we got back home with a beautiful memory of snow.

Since then I have never gone to any places to see the snow, because the snow at Ho Cau Lake has remained in my mind always.
