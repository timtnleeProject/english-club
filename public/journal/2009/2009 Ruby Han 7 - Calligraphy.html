Dec. 17, 2009

Calligraphy

While my daughter was a young child, her teacher asked her to learn calligraphy from an expert who lived nearby. Because I felt bored while waiting for her to finish her calligraphy lesson, I decided to take the same lesson with my daughter.

Unexpectedly, around half a year later, my daughter refused to keep learning it. Instead, my colleagues took the class. We all had a happy time when we wrote calligraphy, followed by eating, chatting and laughing every Saturday afternoon.

After a couple of years, maybe we got bored with it, and so we stopped the class. One of my friends introduced me to two classes of contemporary calligraphy masters in Taipei. I got much improved in the skill of writing calligraphy because I worked hard and wrote a plenty of important ancient models from bronze inscription of Cho Dynasty (鐘鼎文) to regular script of Tang Dynasty (唐楷).

By chance, I represented the school where I taught to attend the Taipei calligraphy contest among teachers and luckily got second place. Afterwards, my works (Picture 1) were selected by the ROC Calligraphy Association several times to be displayed at the Sun Yat-sen Memorial Hall until there was not enough funds to sponsor this meaningful activity. In 2001, the Association chose some works in Taiwan for an oversea exhibition in L.A. Again, I was fortunate enough to have one of my works (Picture 2) exhibited in the US for two weeks.

Now, since I don’t have enough time, I only write calligraphy when someone asks for it. I had written it for my friends, schools, and the Association. While Wenshan Library made its way to build a place of tea culture, they asked me to write calligraphy of a poem about tea for this special section. They carved my calligraphy on the glass (Picture 3) to be set in front of the beautiful artificial scenery.

Some of my friends suggested that I have an exhibition and promised to help me with whatever I need. When I think about the numerous works on display to be dealt with after the exhibition, I instantly lose my courage. I suppose if I get to buy another big house with enough space to keep all my art works, maybe I’ll try to give an exhibition of my works someday.
